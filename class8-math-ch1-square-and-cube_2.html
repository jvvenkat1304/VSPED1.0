How to use this lesson

You don't need to be “good at maths” to teach this well. Your job is to sit beside your child, run each activity, and ask the questions we suggest. The discovery does the teaching.

🎯 What your child will learn
  • What square numbers (n²) and cube numbers (n³) really are — as shapes, not just multiplication.
  • How to spot a perfect square, and estimate a square root without a calculator.
  • How to use prime factorisation to find square roots and cube roots.
  • That patterns (odd numbers, last digits) turn “hard” sums into quick reasoning.
🗣 How to run it

Go section by section, top to bottom. At each interactive, let your child move the sliders and click first — before you explain. Ask “What do you notice?” and “Can you predict the next one?”. Being a little wrong and then checking is exactly how this chapter is meant to be learned.

Common wobble to expect: children often confuse “2 squared” with “2 × 2 = 4” vs “2 + 2”. The dot-square activity below fixes this instantly, because they can literally see the square. Come back to it whenever they slip.

🔓 The 100 Lockers Puzzle

The chapter opens with this famous puzzle. Run it and watch which lockers are left open — the answer hides a big idea.

There are 100 closed lockers and 100 students. Student 1 toggles every locker (opens them all). Student 2 toggles every 2nd locker. Student 3 every 3rd, and so on up to student 100. “Toggle” means: if it's closed, open it; if it's open, close it.

Student 0
Closed Open Perfect square ★ Being toggled now

💡 Why do only the perfect squares stay open?

👆 Click any locker above to see the reason for that number.

🗣 The core idea (say this out loud together)

Every locker is toggled once for each of its factors. Factors normally come in pairs — for locker 12: (1×12), (2×6), (3×4). Six factors, an even number of toggles → it ends up closed.

A perfect square is different. Locker 36 = 6 × 6, and its factor list is (1×36), (2×18), (3×12), (4×9), and (6×6). That last pair is the square root paired with itself — so it counts as one factor, not two. That gives an odd number of factors, an odd number of toggles, and the locker stays open.

Put another way (this links to the prime-factors activity): a perfect square is the only kind of number whose factors reduce to a single unique pair X × X. Ask your child to click lockers 12 and 36 above and spot the difference.

⬛ What “squared” actually means

A square number is what you get when a number of dots can be arranged into a perfect square. That's where the name comes from.

5 × 5 = 5² = 25

A 5 by 5 square holds 25 dots.

Say it as “five squared”, written 5². The small 2 means “used as a factor twice”.

🍎 Everyday link

Ask your child to arrange 16 grapes (or coins) into a square. They'll make 4 rows of 4. Now try 12 grapes — it won't make a full square. That “doesn't fit” feeling is what “not a perfect square” means.

Where does the word come from?

In Sanskrit, a square number is varga and a cube is ghana. The word “root” comes from the Sanskrit mula, meaning basis or origin — the number a square “grows” from. Babylonian clay tablets listed squares and cubes as far back as 1700 BCE.

➕ Squares are hidden sums of odd numbers

Watch the square grow one L-shaped layer at a time. Each new layer adds the next odd number.

1 = 1 = 1²

Each coloured “L” is the next odd number: 1, 3, 5, 7, … and the running total is always a perfect square.

🗣 The takeaway

1 + 3 + 5 + 7 + … The sum of the first n odd numbers is always n². So the difference between one square and the next is just an odd number — that's why 126² = 125² + 125 + 126 = 15625 + 251 = 15876 without long multiplication.

🔎 Last-digit detective

Perfect squares can only ever end in 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, or 9. So any number ending in 2, 3, 7 or 8 is instantly not a perfect square.

⚠️ Careful

Ending in 0,1,4,5,6,9 is a clue, not a guarantee. 26 ends in 6 but isn't a perfect square. The last digit can only rule numbers out, never confirm. Also: a perfect square has an even number of trailing zeros (100 ✓, 1000 ✗).

√ Square roots by estimation

A square root undoes squaring: if y = x² then x = √y. Even when the answer isn't whole, we can trap it between two squares.

🗣 Worked example (from the book)

√250 lies between √225 = 15 and √256 = 16. Since 250 is much closer to 256 than to 225, √250 ≈ 16 (the true value is about 15.81). Teach the trapping method — it builds real number sense that a calculator hides.

🧩 Roots by prime factorisation

Break a number into its prime factors, then try to split them into equal groups. For a square root you need two identical groups; for a cube root, three.

🗣 The idea in one line

Example: 324 = (2 × 3 × 3) × (2 × 3 × 3), two identical groups, so √324 = 18. And 3375 = (3 × 5) × (3 × 5) × (3 × 5), three identical groups, so ∛3375 = 15. If the primes can't be split evenly, the number isn't a perfect square/cube.

🧊 Cubes: multiplying three times

A cube number stacks into a solid cube: n × n × n = n³. 1, 8, 27, 64, 125, …

3 × 3 × 3 = 3³ = 27

🖱️ Drag the cube to rotate it. It's a solid 3 × 3 × 3 block — length × width × height — made of 27 little unit cubes.

Cubes also hide odd numbers: 1 = 1³,   3+5 = 8 = 2³,   7+9+11 = 27 = 3³.

🚕 The 1729 story

A true story about the Indian mathematician Srinivasa Ramanujan — and a number the chapter loves.

When Ramanujan was ill in hospital, the mathematician G. H. Hardy visited in taxi number 1729 and remarked it seemed a dull number. Ramanujan instantly replied that it was quite the opposite — it is the smallest number expressible as a sum of two cubes in two different ways.

🗣 Why include this

It shows maths as curiosity and delight, not drills — and it's a proud moment of Indian mathematical heritage. Numbers with this property are now called taxicab numbers. Ask your child to verify each sum with the cube stacks above.
